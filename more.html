Previous studies on PJMIF

PJMIF is one of several possible embodiments of magneto-inertial fusion, an emerging class of modern fusion approaches. It is the only embodiment that has the unique combination of standoff implosion and high implosion velocity (50 km/s - 150 km/s). It uses plasma guns for all plasma formation and implosion. It has potential for relatively high repetition rate from 1 to 2 Hz.

Plasma jets for forming liners for near-term experiments in PJMIF needs to be compact, dense, high-velocity, high-Mach-number plasma jets:

- Velocity ~ 50 km/s

- Atomic density > 10^16 per cc

- Mach number > 10

- Jet-to-jet jitter < 200 ns

- Jet-to-jet mass variations < 5%

In magneto-inertial fusion (Figure 1), a magnetized plasma target is imploded by a material shell, called a liner. The target plasma is preheated to a sufficiently elevated temperature, typically ∼ 100−300 eV (about 1 - 3 million degrees Kelvin), so that radial convergence of the liner-on-target implosion may be limited to be less than 10 to limit the degree of hydrodynamic instabilities during the implosion. The liner may be solid, liquid or gaseous, and is used to compress the magnetized plasma target.Sometimes a cold fuel layer is used to boost the fusion gain of the configuration. MIF is indeed a hybrid of magnetic and inertial fusion.

Plasma Gun Design (Patent Application in progress)

The plasma gun, also known as an electromagnetic launcher (or Magnetoplasmadynamic thruster / argon thruster), consists of three main sections: Gun electrode, Gas Valve and Main Capacitor Bank at the rear end. I am responsible for designing the Gas Valve and the Gun electrodes.

The Gas Valve is the key part of the gun. Its working principle is the use of an electromagnetic coil to drive the flyer plate up and down to realize opening and closing functions; in the meantime, a small amout of Argon gas is dispensed into the electrodes, followed by a pre-ionization, and then launched in the form of a hypersonic plasma jet. The plasma gun uses the Lorentz force to generate thrust. The electric charge flowing through the plasma in the presence of a magnetic field causes the plasma to accelerate at a relatively high velocity.

Under the supervision of Prof. Thio, I developed an all-new gas valve design for STG1. The original reciprocating valve is updated to an innovative unidirectional valve. This new design reduces the valve's opening time from 300μs down to 150-200μs based on simulation results and precise Laser Displacement Sensor (by KEYENCE) testing results.

This state-of-the-art plasma gun was designed based on careful production engineering analysis.

Vacuum Chamber System Design

Apart from the plasma gun development, I also designed and set up TWO vacuum chamber systems: one for the Gas Valve & Pre-ionization Platform; the other for the Gun Development Platform. They were commissioned in January 2021 and June 2021 respectively and are now both put into service.

The gas valve & pre-ionization vacuum chamber is a cylindrical high vacuum test facility of 0.86 m in length, 0.5 m in inner diameter and 1 m in laser diagnostics height (centre of window). The Plasma Gun vacuum chamber is a larger cylindrical high vacuum test facility of 2.4 m in length, 1 m in inner diameter and 1 m in laser diagnostics height. They both use a combination of a dry pump and a turbomolecular pump from Edwards [UK] to achieve background pressures of down to 10^(-7) torr for experimental purposes.

The purpose of the vacuum facility is to develop and optimize the state-of-the-art plasma gun design to produce a plasma jet with the optimal firing characteristics.

The two complete vacuum systems were all designed and developed independently by myself, including the main vacuum tank, quartz window, CF/KF/ISO flanges, vacuum gauges (ionization gauge & Pirani gauge), feedthroughs for BNC and optic fibre, vacuum pumps, digital control console, cooling water system, gas supply system, etc.

🔸 🔸 🔸 🔸 🔸 Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) 🔸 🔸 🔸 🔸 🔸

Due to the cost and scale of Conventional reactors, they are 1) Too big to fund 2) Too slow to construct to meet power market needs 3) Too expensive to make nuclear energy competitive; thus, SMRs based on LWR technology are being considered now.

Production engineering plays an important role in SMR Economics. The complete process is first getting stuff into a factory, getting the best tools and techniques, repeating things again and again, and the costs are fallen (also known as fordism). It has nothing to do with the product size (ranging from large steam turbines down to domestic cookers).

Things that are smaller intrinsically cost a bit more, which is called the Diseconomics of small scale (or the Economics of large scale). In the last 40 years, every vendor has tried to solve economic problems by making the same design they had but a bit bigger, from 300MW to 1500MW or more.

Therefore, to deal with SMMRs, we need to offset this issue by investigating the spectrum (relationship) between the factors of scaling and the cost of production engineering.

- Standardization

Designing every power plant as a unique plant incurring lots of on-site design costs and regulatory costs. SO design it once like what we do in factories.

- Modularization

Much better productivity

- Relatively large program

Based on R&D, 300 MW seemed to be a sweet spot between the economics of scale and the ability to modularization

🟢 Hall-effect thruster

A Hall Effect Thruster (HET) is an ion thruster in which electrons emitted by a cathode are trapped in a magnetic field, and used to ionize a propellant – thereby creating a plasma into the plasma chamber. The plasma is then accelerated to produce thrust, thanks to an electric field created between the anode and the cathode. It combines the high impulse of electric propulsion technology along with a very high thrust-to-power ratio.

Xenon is an inert gas. Unlike Iodine, Indium, Titanium, Mercury – Xenon can not react with the spacecraft. It means that the risk of contamination is much lower than with competing technologies.

HET has high flexibility, scalability and adaptability.

Nuclear fusion power and plasma-based rocket propulsion

The most powerful plasma rocket in the world is the Variable Specific Impulse Magnetoplasma Rocket (VASIMR) developed by the Ad Astra Rocket Company in Texas, US.

The VASIMR is a state-of-the-art rocket design that uses plasma for rocket propulsion. The concept was first developed by Dr. Franklin Ramón Chang-Díaz (former NASA astronaut) in 1979. Based on research at MIT and NASA, the VASIMR engine is 'a plasma rocket with 10 times the performance of a chemial rocket, using 1/10th of the fuel'.

Nuclear Economics

As nuclear is often labelled “expensive”, I researched the nuclear’s economy and reviewed authoritative data by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD). In recent years, the US government was following the rule of 'Develop as many as different types of new nuclear power stations' and tried to find the optimal design with the best cost-competitiveness. This is one innovative way. Another way is through downsizing - 'Making things Small and Modular'.

Cost estimates based on the NuScale SMRs and the Westinghouse AP1000 Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR) provide convincing evidence that nuclear energy can improve its cost-competitiveness through constant evolution and standardized replication.

A number of companies, including Britain’s Rolls-Royce and GE Hitachi, are pitching to sell their products. However, the new SMR industry is still facing many challenges, among which cost containment is the biggest concern.

Research by the team led by Prof Tony Roulstone at Cambridge university concluded that if you used the same project techniques as for conventional plants, the SMR would cost (once the interest costs incurred in construction were taken into account) roughly 70 per cent more per kilowatt (kW) to build than the larger one. [Available on FINANCIAL TIMES - The big Challenge facing small nuclear reactors (11 OCT 2020)]
